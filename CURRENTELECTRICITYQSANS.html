Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Questions)

Q1. A steady current flows through a metallic conductor of non-uniform cross-section. Which of the following quantities remains constant along the length of the conductor?
A) Drift velocity
B) Current density
C) Electric current
D) Electric field
Correct Option: C
Explanation: Charge conservation dictates that steady current \(I\) remains identical across all segments. Conversely, current density \(j = \frac{I}{A}\), electric field \(E = \frac{j}{\sigma}\), and drift speed \(v_d = \frac{I}{neA}\) depend directly on the local area and vary.
Q2. A wire of resistance \(R\) is stretched uniformly to double its original length. What is its new electrical resistance?
A) \(R\)
B) \(2R\)
C) \(4R\)
D) \(R/2\)
Correct Option: C
Explanation: Total volume \(V = A \cdot l\) is conserved. If length doubles (\(l' = 2l\)), area is halved (\(A' = \frac{A}{2}\)). \[ R' = \rho \frac{l'}{A'} = \rho \frac{2l}{A/2} = 4R \]
Q3. How does the drift velocity \(v_d\) of free electrons vary with the applied electric field \(E\)?
A) \(v_d \propto \sqrt{E}\)
B) \(v_d \propto E\)
C) \(v_d \propto E^2\)
D) \(v_d\) is independent of \(E\)
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Under standard travel profiles, \(v_d = \left(\frac{e\tau}{m}\right)E\), which directly establishes that \(v_d \propto E\).
Q4. Two copper wires have lengths in the ratio \(1:3\) and areas in the ratio \(3:1\). The ratio of their electrical resistivities is:
A) \(1:1\)
B) \(1:9\)
C) \(9:1\)
D) \(1:3\)
Correct Option: A
Explanation: Resistivity (\(\rho\)) is an intrinsic property. It depends purely on the material type and temperature, not on dimensions.
Q5. With an increase in temperature, the relaxation time (\(\tau\)) of free electrons inside a metal:
A) Increases
B) Decreases
C) Remains constant
D) First increases, then decreases
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Higher temperatures cause lattice ions to vibrate with greater amplitudes, increasing collision frequency and decreasing relaxation time \(\tau\).
Q6. The SI unit of charge carrier mobility (\(\mu\)) is:
A) \(\text{m}^2 \text{V}^{-1} \text{s}^{-1}\)
B) \(\text{m} \text{V}^{-1} \text{s}^{-2}\)
C) \(\Omega \cdot \text{m}\)
D) \(\text{A} \cdot \text{m}^{-2}\)
Correct Option: A
Explanation: Mobility is defined as \(\mu = \frac{v_d}{E}\). Units evaluate to \(\frac{\text{m/s}}{\text{V/m}} = \text{m}^2\text{V}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}\).
Q7. A carbon resistor has color bands: Yellow, Violet, Brown, and Gold. Its nominal resistance value is:
A) \(47 \Omega \pm 5\%\)
B) \(470 \Omega \pm 5\%\)
C) \(4.7 \text{ k}\Omega \pm 10\%\)
D) \(470 \Omega \pm 10\%\)
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Yellow = 4, Violet = 7, Brown = \(10^1\), Gold = \(\pm 5\%\). Combined: \(47 \times 10^1 = 470\,\Omega \pm 5\%\).
Q8. An electric bulb is rated at \(100\text{ W}\) for a \(220\text{ V}\) supply. The filament resistance is:
A) \(220 \Omega\)
B) \(484 \Omega\)
C) \(100 \Omega\)
D) \(242 \Omega\)
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Calculated using \(R = \frac{V^2}{P} = \frac{220 \times 220}{100} = 484\,\Omega\).
Q9. Two bulbs of power \(25\text{ W}\) and \(100\text{ W}\) (\(220\text{ V}\)) are connected in series across a \(220\text{ V}\) line. Which bulb glows brighter?
A) \(100\text{ W}\) bulb
B) \(25\text{ W}\) bulb
C) Both glow with identical brightness
D) Neither bulb will operate
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Resistance \(R = \frac{V^2}{P}\), so \(R_{25\text{W}} > R_{100\text{W}}\). In a series circuit, current \(I\) is constant. Because \(P_{\text{real}} = I^2 R\), the \(25\text{ W}\) bulb dissipates more power and glows brighter.
Q10. An example of a non-ohmic device is:
A) Copper wire
B) Manganin wire
C) Semiconductor diode
D) Nichrome element
Correct Option: C
Explanation: A semiconductor diode displays non-linear, asymmetric current-voltage curves, making it non-ohmic.
Q11.Kirchhoff's first law (\(\sum I = 0\)) at an electrical junction is a direct manifestation of the conservation of:
A) Energy
B) Linear momentum
C) Electric charge
D) Mass
Correct Option: C
Explanation: Charge cannot accumulate at a steady-state junction, so input current must equal output current.
Q12.Kirchhoff's loop rule (\(\sum \Delta V = 0\)) is based on the conservation of:
A) Charge
B) Energy
C) Mass
D) Momentum
Correct Option: B
Explanation: The electrostatic field is conservative. Moving a test charge around any closed loop results in a net work done of zero.
Q13.In a balanced Wheatstone bridge, if the positions of the galvanometer and the battery are interchanged, the balance condition:
A) Changes completely
B) Remains unchanged
C) Becomes invalid
D) Doubles its sensitivity
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Interchanging source and detector branches maintains identical bridge cross-ratio networks (\(\frac{P}{Q}=\frac{R}{S}\)).
Q14.Ten identical cells, each of EMF \(E\) and internal resistance \(r\), are connected in series to form a closed loop. A voltmeter connected across any group of three cells reads:
A) \(3E\)
B) Zero
C) \(7E\)
D) \(10E\)
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Total current is \(I = \frac{10E}{10r} = \frac{E}{r}\). Terminal voltage across three cells is \(V = 3E - 3Ir = 3E - 3\left(\frac{E}{r}\right)r = 0\text{ V}\).
Q15.When two identical cells of EMF \(E\) and internal resistance \(r\) are connected in parallel, the effective internal resistance is:
A) \(2r\)
B) \(r\)
C) \(r/2\)
D) \(2/r\)
Correct Option: C
Explanation: Solved using parallel rules: \(\frac{1}{r_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{r} + \frac{1}{r} = \frac{2}{r} \implies r_{\text{eq}} = \frac{r}{2}\).
Q16.A potentiometer is superior to a standard voltmeter for measuring cell EMF because:
A) It has smaller resistance
B) It draws no current at the null point
C) It calculates values directly
D) It uses alternating current
Correct Option: B
Explanation: By operating at a null-balance point, it avoids pulling current from the test cell, which eliminates internal voltage drops (\(Ir\)) and measures true open-circuit EMF.
Q17.The temperature coefficient of resistance (\(\alpha\)) is negative for which group of materials?
A) Copper, Silver, Aluminium
B) Silicon, Germanium, Carbon
C) Nichrome, Manganin, Constantan
D) Iron, Cobalt, Nickel
Correct Option: B
Explanation: In semiconductors, heat breaks covalent bonds, which increases charge carrier density (\(n\)) exponentially and lowers overall resistance.
Q18.A steady current of \(8\text{ mA}\) flows through a wire. The total number of electrons crossing a cross-section in \(10\text{ seconds}\) is:
A) \(5.0 \times 10^{17}\)
B) \(1.6 \times 10^{19}\)
C) \(6.25 \times 10^{18}\)
D) \(8.0 \times 10^{16}\)
Correct Option: A
Explanation: Derived using \(n = \frac{It}{e} = \frac{(8 \times 10^{-3}) \times 10}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 5.0 \times 10^{17}\).
Q19.Standard standard resistors are constructed using alloys like Manganin primarily because they have:
A) High resistivity and a near-zero temperature coefficient
B) Low resistivity and structural limits
C) Highly non-linear properties
D) Superconducting traits at room temperature
Correct Option: A
Explanation: A near-zero \(\alpha\) ensures resistance values remain highly stable during operational temperature shifts.
Q20.The terminal potential difference \(V\) of a cell with EMF \(E\) and internal resistance \(r\) when delivering current \(I\) is:
A) \(V = E + Ir\)
B) \(V = E - Ir\)
C) \(V = I^2 r\)
D) \(V = E\)
Correct Option: B
Explanation: Delivering current causes a potential drop across the cell's internal resistance, which reduces terminal voltage.

Section C: Objective & Very Short Answer Questions (20 Questions)

Q41.Define electrical current density and state its SI unit.
Current density (\(j\)) is current per unit area perpendicular to flow direction (\(j = \frac{I}{A}\)). Unit: \(\text{A/m}^2\). It is a vector.
Q42.Write the vector form of Ohm's law.
\(\vec{j} = \sigma \vec{E}\), where \(\vec{j}\) is current density, \(\sigma\) is conductivity, and \(\vec{E}\) is electric field.
Q43.What is the net charge on a wire that is carrying an electrical current?
Zero. At any instant, moving negative electrons exactly balance the positive lattice ions.
Q44.How does the electrical conductivity of an electrolyte change with an increase in temperature?
Conductivity increases because higher temperatures decrease solvent viscosity, which increases ion mobility.
Q45.State the mathematical formula relating mobility (\(\mu\)) and relaxation time (\(\tau\)).
\(\mu = \frac{e\tau}{m}\), where \(e\) is electronic charge and \(m\) is electron mass.
Q46.What is the current through the central galvanometer branch of a balanced Wheatstone bridge?
Zero (\(I_g = 0\)). The two bridge nodes across the arm share equal potentials.
Q47.How does semiconductor resistivity alter with absolute temperature parameters?
It decreases exponentially because thermal energy breaks covalent bonds and generates more active charge carriers.
Q48.If the potential difference across a given conductor is doubled while keeping its temperature constant, how is the drift velocity affected?
Since \(v_d \propto E\) and \(E = \frac{V}{l}\), doubling the voltage doubles the internal field, which doubles the drift velocity.
Q49.What is the electrical resistance of an ideal voltmeter?
Infinite (\(\infty\,\Omega\)), which ensures it draws zero parallel current from the circuit under test.
Q50.What is the electrical resistance of an ideal ammeter?
Zero (\(0\,\Omega\)), which prevents it from causing a voltage drop when placed in series.
Q51.Name two materials that have a near-zero temperature coefficient of resistance.
Manganin and Constantan (Eureka).
Q52.Identify the terminal condition of a cell when its terminal voltage \(V\) equals its electromotive force \(E\).
Open-circuit state (\(I = 0\)). The battery is not delivering or receiving any current.
Q53.How does the relaxation time of electrons in a metal change when it is cooled down?
Relaxation time (\(\tau\)) increases because lower temperatures suppress lattice vibrations, reducing collision frequency.
Q54.What is the principal conservation law underlying Kirchhoff's Loop Rule?
The Law of Conservation of Energy.
Q55.State the condition under which a cell delivers maximum power to an external load resistor \(R\).
When the external load resistance matches the cell's internal resistance (\(R = r\)).
Q56.Why is the wire of a slide meter bridge made of uniform cross-section?
To maintain uniform resistance per unit length, allowing wire resistance to scale linearly with length (\(R \propto l\)).
Q57.Express electrical power density \(P/V\) in terms of current density \(j\) and resistivity \(\rho\).
Power density \(= j^2 \rho\), derived from: \(\frac{I^2 R}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{(jA)^2 (\rho l / A)}{Al} = j^2 \rho\).
Q58.What is the main advantage of a null-point method?
It isolates the measurement from contact resistances or circuit loading because no current flows through the detection branch at balance.
Q59.Can terminal potential difference exceed a cell's rated EMF? If so, when?
Yes, when a cell is being charged by an external power source that drives current into the positive terminal: \(V = E + Ir\).
Q60.What physical quantity is measured by the slope of a linear \(V-I\) graph?
The electrical resistance (\(R\)) of the conductor.

Section D: Short Answer Type I Questions (20 Questions)

Q61.Derive the basic relationship between electric current \(I\) and electron drift velocity \(v_d\).
Consider a conductor of length \(l\) and area \(A\). Let \(n\) be the free electron density.
Total volume \(= A \cdot l\). Total mobile charge \(Q = nAle\).
Time required for charge to clear the length: \(t = \frac{l}{v_d}\).
Using the definition of current: \[ I = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{nAle}{l/v_d} = neAv_d \]
Q62.A heating element made of Nichrome is connected to a \(230\text{ V}\) supply and draws an initial current of \(3.2\text{ A}\), which soon settles to a steady value of \(2.8\text{ A}\). If the room temperature is \(27.0^\circ\text{C}\), find the steady temperature of the element. (Take \(\alpha = 1.70 \times 10^{-4}\text{ }^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\)).
Calculate initial and final resistances:
\(R_0 = \frac{230}{3.2} = 71.875\,\Omega\) and \(R_1 = \frac{230}{2.8} = 82.143\,\Omega\).
Using \(R_1 = R_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]\):
\[ \frac{82.143}{71.875} - 1 = \alpha(T - 27) \implies 0.14285 = (1.70 \times 10^{-4})(T - 27) \]
\[ T - 27 = \frac{0.14285}{1.70 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 840.3^\circ\text{C} \implies T \approx 867.3^\circ\text{C} \]
Q63.Explain why terminal potential difference drops when a cell supplies current to a circuit.
When current flows, ions encounter resistance moving through the electrolyte. This internal resistance (\(r\)) creates an internal voltage drop equal to \(Ir\), which reduces terminal voltage: \(V = E - Ir\).
Q64.A wire with a resistance of \(16\,\Omega\) is bent into a closed circle. Calculate the effective resistance between any two diametrically opposite points.
The circle forms two semicircular paths in parallel, each with a resistance of \(\frac{16}{2} = 8\,\Omega\). \[ R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{8 \times 8}{8 + 8} = 4\,\Omega \]
Q65.Distinguish between electrical resistivity and electrical conductivity. State their relationship.
Resistivity (\(\rho\)) measures a material's intrinsic opposition to current, whereas conductivity (\(\sigma\)) measures its intrinsic ability to conduct. They are reciprocals: \(\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}\).
Q66.Why are copper and aluminium preferred for house wiring?
They combine excellent electrical conductivity with flexibility and low cost, which minimizes energy losses (\(I^2 R\)) across grid nodes.
Q67.Why do gases require high-energy radiation like X-rays to conduct electricity?
Neutral gas molecules lack free charge carriers. High-energy radiation ionizes the gas atoms, releasing free electrons and ions to conduct current.
Q68.State Ohm's law and its main structural limitation.
\(V \propto I\) provided physical conditions like temperature remain constant. It fails under significant self-heating or inside semiconductor layouts.
Q69.What is a thermistor? State one application.
A temperature-sensitive semiconductor resistor with a high temperature coefficient. Commonly used for temperature sensing and thermal overload protection.
Q70.Resistors \(1\,\Omega, 2\,\Omega, 3\,\Omega\) are in series across \(12\text{ V}\). Find current.
\(R_{\text{eq}} = 1+2+3 = 6\,\Omega \implies I = \frac{12}{6} = 2\text{ A}\).
Q71.Why is a potentiometer wire preferred over a standard voltmeter for cell calibration?
It operates at a null point where no current flows, preventing internal voltage drops and ensuring an accurate open-circuit EMF measurement.
Q72.Define temperature coefficient of resistance and state its unit.
The fractional change in resistance per unit change in temperature (\(\alpha = \frac{\Delta R}{R_0 \Delta T}\)). Unit: \(\text{K}^{-1}\) or \(^\circ\text{C}^{-1}\).
Q73.A wire of resistance \(R\) is melted and recast to half its original radius. Find new resistance.
Volume is conserved. Radius \(\rightarrow \frac{r}{2} \implies \text{Area} \rightarrow \frac{A}{4} \implies \text{Length} \rightarrow 4l\). Therefore, \(R' = \rho \frac{4l}{A/4} = 16R\).
Q74.Explain how a cell's internal resistance changes over its operating lifespan.
It increases over time due to electrolyte depletion and chemical accumulation on the electrode surfaces, which restricts ion movement.
Q75.What is negative dynamic resistance? Give an example of a device that exhibits it.
A region where current decreases as voltage increases (\(\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta I} < 0\)). Observed in tunnel diodes and GaAs semiconductor systems.
Q76.Compare electron drift speed with random thermal speed inside a metal.
Thermal speed is large (\(\sim 10^5\text{ m/s}\)) but completely random, producing zero net flow. Drift speed is small (\(\sim 10^{-4}\text{ m/s}\)) but directional under an electric field, constituting net current.
Q77.Calculate the number of electrons passing per second through a \(100\,\Omega\) resistor across \(200\text{ V}\).
\(I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{200}{100} = 2\text{ A}\). Electrons per second: \(n = \frac{I}{e} = \frac{2}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 1.25 \times 10^{19}\).
Q78.State the network condition required to apply Kirchhoff's rules.
Applies to networks containing lumped components where charge does not build up at nodes and current configurations are steady.
Q79.Why do secondary cells yield higher currents than primary cells?
Secondary cells feature larger electrode surface areas placed closer together in highly conductive electrolytes, which minimizes internal resistance.
Q80.Why is resistance additive in a series configuration loop?
The same current \(I\) flows through each component, so the total voltage drop is the sum of individual drops: \(V = \sum I R_i = I \sum R_i \implies R_{\text{eq}} = \sum R_i\).

Section E: Short Answer Type II Questions (20 Questions)

Q81.Deduce Ohm's law from microscopic electron mechanics and derive an expression for material resistivity (\(\rho\)).
We begin with current \(I = neAv_d\) and electron acceleration \(a = \frac{eE}{m}\).
Drift velocity is given by \(v_d = a\tau = \frac{eE\tau}{m}\). Substituting \(E = \frac{V}{l}\):
\[ v_d = \frac{eV\tau}{ml} \implies I = neA\left(\frac{eV\tau}{ml}\right) = \left(\frac{ne^2A\tau}{ml}\right)V \]
Rearranging for voltage yields: \[ V = \left(\frac{m}{ne^2\tau} \cdot \frac{l}{A}\right)I \ Pand \] Comparing this with Ohm's law (\(V = IR\)) shows that \(R = \frac{m}{ne^2\tau}\frac{l}{A}\). Since \(R = \rho\frac{l}{A}\), resistivity is expressed as: \[ \rho = \frac{m}{ne^2\tau} \]
Q82.Two cells of EMFs \(E_1, E_2\) and internal resistances \(r_1, r_2\) are connected in parallel. Derive expressions for equivalent EMF and resistance.
Let terminal voltage be \(V\). The individual branch currents are:
\(I_1 = \frac{E_1 - V}{r_1}\) and \(I_2 = \frac{E_2 - V}{r_2}\).
Total current \(I = I_1 + I_2\): \[ I = \frac{E_1 - V}{r_1} + \frac{E_2 - V}{r_2} = \left(\frac{E_1}{r_1} + \frac{E_2}{r_2}\right) - V\left(\frac{1}{r_1} + \frac{1}{r_2}\right) \]
Rearranging for \(V\) yields: \[ V = \frac{\frac{E_1}{r_1} + \frac{E_2}{r_2}}{\frac{1}{r_1} + \frac{1}{r_2}} - I\left(\frac{1}{\frac{1}{r_1} + \frac{1}{r_2}}\right) \]
Comparing this with the standard format \(V = E_{\text{eq}} - Ir_{\text{eq}}\) gives: \[ E_{\text{eq}} = \frac{E_1r_2 + E_2r_1}{r_1 + r_2}, \quad r_{\text{eq}} = \frac{r_1r_2}{r_1 + r_2} \]
Q83.A network of 12 identical resistors, each of resistance \(1\,\Omega\), forms the edges of a cube. A \(10\text{ V}\) battery is connected across diagonally opposite corners. Find equivalent resistance using Kirchhoff's rules.
Assume a total current of \(6I\) enters the input corner. By symmetry, it branches equally into three edges (\(2I\) each) meeting at that junction.
At the next set of nodes, each path splits into two directions, so those edges carry a current of \(I\). At the exit corner, the currents recombine into three paths of \(2I\) each, reconstructing the total current of \(6I\).
Apply Kirchhoff's loop rule along any path from the input corner to the diagonally opposite corner: \[ V = 2IR + IR + 2IR = 5IR \ Pand \]
Since total resistance satisfies \(V = 6I \cdot R_{\text{eq}}\), equating the expressions yields: \[ 6I \cdot R_{\text{eq}} = 5IR \implies R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{5}{6}R \]
Given \(R = 1\,\Omega\), the equivalent resistance is \(R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{5}{6}\,\Omega\).
Q84.Explain the operation of a meter bridge to determine unknown resistance.
Operates on the Wheatstone bridge principle. A sliding jockey locates a null deflection point at length \(l\) along a uniform wire, establishing the balance condition: \(\frac{X}{R} = \frac{l}{100-l} \implies X = R\frac{l}{100-l}\).
Q85.Derive thermal heat generation based on Joule's laws of heating.
The work done moving a charge is \(dW = VdQ = VIdt\). By energy conservation, this electrical energy is transferred to the lattice as heat: \(dH = VIdt = I^2Rdt \implies H = I^2Rt\).
Q86.A potentiometer wire has length \(4\text{ m}\) and resistance \(8\,\Omega\). It is connected in series with a \(2\text{ V}\) accumulator and a resistor of \(12\,\Omega\). Find the potential gradient.
\(R_{\text{total}} = 8 + 12 = 20\,\Omega \implies I_{\text{primary}} = \frac{2}{20} = 0.1\text{ A}\).
Voltage across wire: \(V_{\text{wire}} = 0.1 \times 8 = 0.8\text{ V}\). Potential gradient: \(k = \frac{0.8}{4} = 0.2\text{ V/m}\).
Q87.Describe an experiment to evaluate cell internal resistance via a potentiometer.
Find the open-circuit balance length \(l_1\) to determine EMF (\(E \propto l_1\)). Shunt the cell with a known resistance \(R\) and locate the new balance length \(l_2\) to determine terminal voltage (\(V \propto l_2\)). This yields internal resistance: \(r = R\left(\frac{l_1}{l_2}-1\right)\).
Q88.Why is alloy resistivity less sensitive to temperature changes than pure metal resistivity?
Alloys have structurally disordered crystal lattices, which creates high baseline resistance from impurity scattering. This baseline scattering dominates over thermal lattice vibrations, meaning heating has a negligible effect on relaxation time (\(\tau\)).
Q89.Three identical cells (\(2\text{ V}, 0.3\,\Omega\)) in parallel drive a \(1.9\,\Omega\) load. Find current.
\(E_{\text{eq}} = 2\text{ V}\), \(r_{\text{eq}} = \frac{0.3}{3} = 0.1\,\Omega\). Total current: \(I = \frac{E_{\text{eq}}}{R + r_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{2}{1.9 + 0.1} = 1\text{ A}\).
Q90.Derive conditions for maximum current from mixed groups (\(m\) rows, \(n\) cells per row).
Current is \(I = \frac{mnE}{mR + nr}\). The denominator can be rewritten as \((\sqrt{mR}-\sqrt{nr})^2 + 2\sqrt{mnRr}\). To maximize current, set the squared term to zero: \(\sqrt{mR} = \sqrt{nr} \implies R = \frac{nr}{m}\) (load resistance matches total internal resistance).
Q91.Why are bridge connecting strips constructed from thick copper elements?
Thick copper elements minimize lead resistance and contact errors, keeping bridge measurements accurate.
Q92.A wire is compressed to decrease length by \(10\%\) at constant volume. Find change in resistance.
\(l' = 0.9l \implies A' = \frac{A}{0.9}\) since volume is conserved. \(R' = \rho \frac{0.9l}{A/0.9} = 0.81R\). This represents a \(19\%\) decrease in resistance.
Q93.What are end errors in bridge setups? How are they countered?
Errors caused by contact resistance at copper joints and alignment shifts at wire ends. They are minimized by swapping the positions of resistors (\(X\) and \(R\)) and averaging the values.
Q94.Find power dissipated in a \(4\,\Omega\) resistor across a cell (\(12\text{ V}, 2\,\Omega\)).
\(I = \frac{12}{4+2} = 2\text{ A} \implies P = I^2R = 2^2 \times 4 = 16\text{ W}\).
Q95.Why does a gas discharge tube display non-ohmic profiles?
Increasing voltage triggers rapid ionization, which exponentially increases charge carrier density and alters resistance across the plasma column.
Q96.Find electron drift speed in a wire with \(n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}\text{ m}^{-3}\), area \(2 \times 10^{-6}\text{ m}^2\), carrying \(3\text{ A}\).
\(v_d = \frac{I}{neA} = \frac{3.0}{8.5 \times 10^{28} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 2 \times 10^{-6}} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m/s}\).
Q97.Describe how to compare cell EMF values using a potentiometer.
Find the balance length for each cell alternately. Since \(E_1 = kl_1\) and \(E_2 = kl_2\), dividing the expressions cancels the potential gradient and yields: \(\frac{E_1}{E_2} = \frac{l_1}{l_2}\).
Q98.An electric kettle boils water in 10 mins using coil 1, and 20 mins using coil 2. Find time required if both are connected in parallel.
From \(H = \frac{V^2}{R}t\), we get \(R_1 \propto 10\) and \(R_2 \propto 20\). In parallel, \(\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} \implies \frac{1}{t_{\text{parallel}}} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{20} = \frac{3}{20} \implies t_{\text{parallel}} = \frac{20}{3} \approx 6.67\text{ mins}\).
Q99.Why is charging potential difference higher than rated EMF?
The external supply forces current into the positive terminal. The applied voltage must overcome the cell's internal EMF plus its internal resistance drop: \(V = E + Ir\).
Q100.State how charge conservation leads directly to Kirchhoff's first law.
In a steady state, electric charge cannot accumulate or dissipate at a junction node. Therefore, the total current entering a junction must equal the total current leaving it, meaning \(\sum I = 0\).

Section F: Case Study-Based Questions (4 Passages, 20 Sub-parts)

Case Study 1: Microscopic Transport Theory
Free electrons inside metals exhibit random thermal motion. When an external electric field is applied, it exerts a steady electrostatic force that gives electrons a directional acceleration. Frequent collisions with vibrating lattice ions continually reset this directional motion. The net resulting movement is a slow drift velocity (\(v_d\)), and the average time between consecutive collisions is defined as the relaxation time (\(\tau\)).
Q101.What is the typical order of magnitude of electron drift velocity?
\(\sim 10^{-4}\text{ m/s}\).
Q102.How does relaxation time respond to an increase in lattice temperature?
It decreases because stronger lattice vibrations increase the frequency of electron-ion collisions.
Q103.Why is net current zero without an external field?
Thermal motion is completely random, meaning electron velocity vectors cancel out symmetrically in all directions.
Q104.Write the equation for drift velocity in terms of relaxation time and electric field.
\(v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m}\).
Q105.Explain why material resistivity is inversely proportional to relaxation time.
Resistivity \(\rho = \frac{m}{ne^2\tau}\). Shorter relaxation times indicate more frequent collisions, which increases opposition to electron flow.
Case Study 2: Real Energy Sources & Internal Resistances
Chemical cells maintain potential differences via internal redox pathways. Open-circuit voltage yields true electromotive force (\(E\)), but delivering current creates internal voltage drops across the electrolyte's internal resistance (\(r\)). Terminal voltage is given by \(V = E - Ir\).
Q106.State when terminal voltage \(V\) exactly matches EMF \(E\).
When no current is drawn from the cell (\(I=0\)).
Q107.Calculate short-circuit current for a cell with \(E = 6\text{ V}\) and \(r = 1\,\Omega\).
\(I_{\text{sc}} = \frac{E}{r} = \frac{6}{1} = 6\text{ A}\).
Q108.State two structural parameters affecting cell internal resistance.
The surface area of the electrodes and the distance between electrodes in the electrolyte.
Q109.What does the slope of a terminal voltage \(V\) versus current \(I\) plot represent?
The negative of the cell's internal resistance (\(-r\)).
Q110.State the voltage equation when a cell is being charged.
\(V = E + Ir\).
Case Study 3: Bridge Balances & Core Metric Variants
The Wheatstone Bridge uses an arm resistance ratio loop to evaluate unknown electrical components. The slide meter bridge uses a one-meter uniform wire where a sliding jockey locates a null-balance point along the scale.
Q111.State the standard balance cross-ratio for a Wheatstone bridge.
\(\frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S}\).
Q112.Why is a middle balance position preferred in slide setups?
It maximizes measurement sensitivity and minimizes errors from end contact resistances.
Q113.Find unknown \(X\) if balance length is \(40\text{ cm}\) using standard resistance \(R = 10\,\Omega\).
\(X = R\frac{l}{100-l} = 10 \times \frac{40}{60} = 6.67\,\Omega\).
Q114.Does altering driver battery voltage shift the balance point?
No. The balance point depends strictly on the resistance ratio of the bridge arms, independent of line voltage.
Q115.Why are bridge wires made of alloy compositions like Constantan?
Alloys combine high resistivity with a low temperature coefficient, keeping wire resistance stable during operation.
Case Study 4: Potentiometer Mechanics
Potentiometers measure potential profiles using a null-balance method that draws zero current. A primary driver cell sets up a uniform potential drop per unit length along the wire, known as the potential gradient (\(k = V/L\)).
Q116.Define potential gradient.
The potential drop per unit length along the potentiometer wire (\(k = \frac{V}{L}\)).
Q117.State how to increase the measurement sensitivity of a potentiometer.
Sensitivity can be increased by reducing the potential gradient, either by increasing total wire length or decreasing primary current.
Q118.Cell 1 balances at \(250\text{ cm}\) and Cell 2 balances at \(200\text{ cm}\). Find their EMF ratio.
\(\frac{E_1}{E_2} = \frac{l_1}{l_2} = \frac{250}{200} = 1.25\).
Q119.Why will a potentiometer fail to balance if the test cell EMF exceeds the primary wire voltage drop?
The wire cannot provide an opposing potential large enough to cancel the test cell's voltage, making it impossible to bring galvanometer current to zero.
Q120.Why must all positive terminals connect to the same zero-end node?
This alignment ensures that the potential drop along the wire opposes the test cell's EMF, which is necessary to achieve a null balance.

Section G: Long Answer Questions (20 Questions)

Q121.State Kirchhoff's network rules. Detail the analytical layout strategies used to parse multi-loop formats, stating core physical foundations.
1. Kirchhoff's First Law (Junction Rule): The algebraic sum of currents meeting at any network node is zero (\(\sum I = 0\)). Currents entering a junction are designated positive, and currents exiting are negative. Physical Foundation: Law of Conservation of Charge.

2. Kirchhoff's Second Law (Loop Rule): The algebraic sum of potential changes around any closed circuit loop is zero (\(\sum \Delta V = 0\)). Physical Foundation: Law of Conservation of Energy.

Analytical Loop Strategies:
• Assign current paths to each branch and use the Junction Rule at nodes to minimize variables.
• Choose a clockwise or counter-clockwise path to traverse the loops.
• Sign Convention: (a) Traversing a resistor in the direction of current creates a potential drop (\(-IR\)); traversing against current creates a potential rise (\(+IR\)). (b) Passing through a cell from negative to positive terminal adds a potential rise (\(+E\)); passing from positive to negative terminal creates a drop (\(-E\)).
• Construct the loop linear equations and solve for the unknown currents.
Q122.Derive the balanced cross-product verification equations for a Wheatstone bridge network using Kirchhoff's rules.
Consider four bridge arm resistors \(P, Q, R, S\) forming a diamond loop, with a central galvanometer branch of resistance \(G\).
Let total current \(I\) split into \(I_1\) through arm \(P\) and \(I_2\) through arm \(R\). At the top node, current \(I_g\) branches through the galvanometer, leaving (\(I_1 - I_g\)) to pass through arm \(Q\). The currents recombine at the bottom node, sending (\(I_2 + I_g\)) through arm \(S\).
Apply Kirchhoff's Loop Rule to the left loop: \[ -I_1P - I_gG + I_2R = 0 \]
Apply the Loop Rule to the right loop: \[ -(I_1 - I_g)Q + (I_2 + I_g)S + I_gG = 0 \]
At balance, no current flows through the galvanometer branch (\(I_g = 0\)). This simplifies the equations to: \[ I_1P = I_2R \implies \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{R}{P} \]
\[ I_1Q = I_2S \implies \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{S}{Q} \]
Equating the current ratios yields the final bridge balance condition: \[ \frac{R}{P} = \frac{S}{Q} \implies \frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S} \]
Q123.Explain the operating principle of a potentiometer. Detail how it can compare cell EMFs and determine internal resistance.
Operating Principle: When a steady current flows through a wire of uniform cross-section, the potential drop across any segment is directly proportional to its length (\(V \propto l \implies V = kl\)), where \(k\) is the potential gradient.

Comparing EMFs: Connect two test cells to the wire through a two-way switch. Find the null balance length for each cell alternately. Since the bridge draws zero current at balance, the wire's potential drop matches the cell's open-circuit EMF exactly: \[ E_1 = kl_1 \quad \text{and} \quad E_2 = kl_2 \implies \frac{E_1}{E_2} = \frac{l_1}{l_2} \]
Determining Internal Resistance: First, find the balance length \(l_1\) with the cell on an open circuit (\(E = kl_1\)). Next, close a shunt switch to connect a known external resistance \(R\) across the cell, and find the new balance length \(l_2\), which measures terminal voltage (\(V = kl_2\)). Using the internal resistance relationship \(r = R\left(\frac{E}{V}-1\right)\), we can substitute the lengths to find: \[ r = R\left(\frac{l_1}{l_2} - 1\right) \]
Q124.Derive equations for equivalent EMF and resistance for cells connected in series and parallel. Discuss when each is preferred.
Series: \(E_{\text{eq}} = nE, r_{\text{eq}} = nr\). Preferred when external load resistance is much larger than internal resistance (\(R \gg nr\)). Parallel: \(E_{\text{eq}} = E, r_{\text{eq}} = \frac{r}{n}\). Preferred when external load resistance is much smaller than internal resistance (\(R \ll r\)).
Q125.Derive the microscopic equation \(v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m}\) and detail how temperature affects resistivity factors.
Electric field forces accelerate free electrons (\(a = \frac{eE}{m}\)). Collisions with lattice ions reset this motion every relaxation interval \(\tau\), giving a directional drift speed: \(v_d = a\tau = \frac{eE\tau}{m}\). Substituting this into current equations gives resistivity: \(\rho = \frac{m}{ne^2\tau}\). Heating increases lattice vibrations, shortening \(\tau\) and raising resistivity.
Q126.A potential difference \(V\) is applied across a wire of length \(l\) and area \(A\). Analyze how drift speed and resistance respond if: (a) \(V\) is doubled, (b) \(l\) is halved, or (c) the wire is stretched to double its length.
(a) Doubling \(V\) doubles electric field, which doubles drift speed; resistance is unchanged. (b) Halving length doubles electric field and drift speed; resistance is halved. (c) Stretching to double length increases resistance fourfold (\(4R\)) because area is halved. Under a constant voltage, the electric field and drift speed are halved.
Q127.Contrast primary and secondary chemical cells across mechanical layouts, operating efficiencies, and industrial deployments.
Primary cells rely on irreversible chemical paths, have higher internal resistance, and cannot be recharged (e.g., zinc-carbon cells in remotes). Secondary cells use reversible reactions, have low internal resistance, and can be recharged efficiently (e.g., lead-acid or Li-ion batteries in vehicles and phones).
Q128.State Joule's heating laws. Explain how power lines minimize line losses during long-distance transmission.
\(P = I^2R\). Line heating is minimized by using transformers to step up transmission lines to high voltages. This significantly reduces the current (\(I\)) required to transmit a given amount of power, minimizing energy losses (\(I^2R\)) along the long-distance lines.
Q129.What is potentiometer sensitivity? Explain how it can be adjusted experimentally.
Sensitivity is a potentiometer's ability to resolve small voltage differences, which requires a low potential gradient (\(k\)). Experimentally, sensitivity is increased by extending the total wire length or using a series rheostat to reduce primary circuit current.
Q130.Derive a general efficiency equation for power delivery from a real battery to a variable external load \(R\).
Total power is \(P_{\text{total}} = EI\), and useful load power is \(P_{\text{useful}} = I^2R\). Efficiency is given by:
\(\eta = \frac{P_{\text{useful}}}{P_{\text{total}}} = \frac{I^2R}{EI} = \frac{IR}{E} = \frac{\left(\frac{E}{R+r}\right)R}{E} = \frac{R}{R+r} = \frac{1}{1 + r/R}\). Efficiency approaches \(100\%\) when \(R \gg r\).
Q131.Define charge mobility. Derive its relationship with electrical conductivity in a material containing multiple types of charge carriers.
\(\mu = \frac{v_d}{E}\). Since \(j = nev_d = ne\mu E\) and Ohm's law states \(j = \sigma E\), conductivity is \(\sigma = ne\mu\). For multiple carrier types (such as electrons and holes in semiconductors), conductivity combines additively: \(\sigma = e(n_e\mu_e + n_h\mu_h)\).
Q132.Explain why non-linear \(V-I\) parameters occur in semiconductor layouts based on charge carrier dynamics.
Unlike metals, where free electron density is constant, an applied voltage across a semiconductor can alter its internal carrier concentration (\(n\)) by breaking covalent bonds or injecting carriers across junctions, which causes resistance to vary with voltage.
Q133.Detail the mathematical conversions required to adapt a standard galvanometer into a high-range voltmeter and ammeter.
Voltmeter conversion: Connect a large resistance \(R\) in series with the galvanometer: \(R = \frac{V}{I_g} - G\). Ammeter conversion: Connect a small shunt resistance \(S\) in parallel with the galvanometer: \(S = \frac{I_gG}{I - I_g}\).
Q134.What causes contact resistance at bridge junctions? How does it affect measurements, and how is it minimized?
Contact resistance is caused by surface roughness, oxidation layers, or loose joints at connections. This introduces unintended resistance into the bridge arms, which can skew balance point calculations. It is minimized by using thick, clean copper strips and repeating measurements after swapping components.
Q135.Explain standard cell stabilization mechanisms. Why must reference standard cells maintain low discharge currents?
Standard reference cells use specific chemical combinations (like cadmium and mercury) to maintain a highly stable, reproducible EMF. They must maintain near-zero discharge currents during measurements because drawing current triggers internal reactions and concentration polarization that destabilize the reference voltage.
Q136.Discuss how structural lattice defects and chemical impurity doping modify conductor resistivity.
Defects, dislocations, and impurities disrupt the regular periodicity of the crystal lattice. These irregularities act as additional scattering sites for drifting electrons, which shortens the mean relaxation time (\(\tau\)) and increases the material's resistivity.
Q137.Derive the total equivalent resistance for a complex network of nested parallel resistor groups connected in series.
First, calculate the equivalent resistance for each parallel group using the reciprocal rule: \(R_p = \left(\sum \frac{1}{R_i}\right)^{-1}\). Next, substitute these simplified values back into the circuit and sum them linearly to find the total series resistance: \(R_{\text{total}} = \sum R_{\text{series\_elements}} + \sum R_{p\_groups}\).
Q138.Explain thermal stability in electrical instrumentation. Contrast the performance of Manganin versus Copper under operating conditions.
Thermal stability is an instrument's ability to maintain stable electrical properties as operating temperatures vary. Copper has a high temperature coefficient (\(\alpha \approx 3.9 \times 10^{-3}\text{ K}^{-1}\)), meaning its resistance shifts significantly with temperature changes. Manganin has a near-zero coefficient (\(\alpha \approx 10^{-5}\text{ K}^{-1}\)), keeping its resistance stable even when heated by operating currents.
Q139.Why are electric current densities non-uniform across the cross-sections of non-cylindrical or bent wires?
Electric field lines bend to follow the geometry of the conductor, crowding together along shorter inner curves or narrower regions. Since current density maps directly to the local electric field (\(\vec{j} = \sigma \vec{E}\)), this uneven field distribution results in varying current densities.
Q140.State the maximum power transfer theorem for DC networks. Prove it mathematically, and explain why power utilities avoid operating at this point.
Theorem Statement: A power source delivers maximum power to an external load resistor \(R\) when the load resistance matches the source's internal resistance (\(R = r\)).

Proof: Power delivered to the load is \(P = I^2R = \frac{E^2R}{(R+r)^2}\). To find the value of \(R\) that maximizes power, take the derivative with respect to \(R\) and set it to zero: \[ \frac{dP}{dR} = E^2 \left[ \frac{(R+r)^2(1) - R \cdot 2(R+r)}{(R+r)^4} \right] = 0 \implies (R+r) - 2R = 0 \implies R = r \]

Utility Analysis: Power utilities avoid operating at this point because when \(R = r\), exactly half the generated energy is lost as heat within the source's internal resistance, giving a low electrical efficiency of only \(50\%\). This high internal heat loss could also damage or destroy generator equipment. Instead, grids operate with large load resistances (\(R \gg r\)) to maximize energy efficiency.
